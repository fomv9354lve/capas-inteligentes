# CAPAS Governance — Operationalization Pack

*Companion to `GOVERNANCE.md`. The charter is already a binding-on-publication, irrevocable
**statement of direction**: it pre-commits the CAPAS name/mark to neutral governance before adoption,
irrevocably renounces relicensing (§1, §3), and defines the trigger/transfer terms (§4) on the
Styra/OPA → CNCF pattern (§7–§8). What it is **not yet** is **operative**: §2 still carries the literal
placeholder `[TRUSTEE — to be named]`, no escrow/assignment instrument has been executed with counsel,
and the §4 abandonment cure period is unconfirmed (the three "Open items" in the charter's closing note).*

*This pack closes the **paper** half of that gap. It delivers, as decision-ready drafts for counsel:
(1) the trustee designation — who holds the mark and why; (2) the escrow/conditional-assignment instrument
that wires trigger → transfer; and (3) the §4 abandonment cure period made objective. It is a
**draft for counsel**, not executed legal advice. The single remaining real-world act — a human executing
the instrument with counsel and a named trustee — is named explicitly in the closing section, and tracked
operationally in `docs/ADOPTION_GATE_READINESS_LEDGER.md`.*

> **Charter citation conventions used throughout (so no reader is misled about the charter's structure):**
> The charter's §4 is a **single section containing a 1–4 numbered list**, not formal §4.x subsections.
> Where this pack writes "§4, item 1/2/3/4" it means those list items; "T1–T4" is this pack's shorthand
> label for them. Self-run conformance is **§5**; the mark-usage grant is **§6**. The charter's closing

# Part A — Trustee designation: neutrality criteria + named holder form

*Resolves charter Open item (1): the §2 placeholder `[TRUSTEE — to be named]`. This part delivers
**who holds the mark and why** — it does not execute the instrument that effects the holding (Part B). It
(a) fixes the objective neutrality criteria any admissible holder must satisfy, (b) enumerates the
admissible vehicle classes consistent with §2's "executable-today" posture, (c) tests each against §4's
no-consent transfer power and §2's foundation destination, and (d) returns a concrete designation as a
ranked, defensible shortlist with the trigger to convert the interim holder into the §2 destination.*

## A.0 The constraint this part must satisfy

GOVERNANCE.md §2 commits the mark to **neutral governance _before_ adoption**, via a **trustee-escrow
vehicle** that is **executable today** (explicitly "the Certified-Kubernetes pattern without waiting on a
foundation"), with **assignment to an independent foundation as the migration destination**. §4 requires
the holder to execute transfer on a trigger event **without the commercial entity's consent**. §8 anchors
the precedent in Certified Kubernetes / a neutral body owning the mark; §7's Styra/OPA row states the
operative lesson — *the standard survived because CNCF, not Styra, held the mark.*

The present commercial entity is a **single natural person**: per `NOTICE`, copyright and authorship vest
in *Fco. Osvaldo Morales Vilchis (Krenn·IQ / KRENIQ)*. This is the single most important fact for trustee
selection, and it sharpens every criterion below:

- **Concentration risk is maximal.** Entity-owns-engine + entity-owns-mark + entity-controls-its-own-trust
  is the §7 capture configuration in its purest form — here all three sit in one person. The point of
  naming a trustee now is to break that concentration *before* there is adoption value worth capturing.
- **Longevity-beyond-the-entity is not abstract.** The §4 item-3 "abandonment" trigger and the §2 survival
  rationale ("the entity may be sold, pivot, or die") are, for a solo author, also a **succession /
  key-person / incapacity** problem. The trustee must outlive a single individual.
- **"Executable today" is doing real work.** A solo author cannot self-incorporate a credible neutral
  foundation overnight; §2 already concedes this by choosing trustee-escrow as the *interim* vehicle with
  foundation assignment as the *destination*. So the named holder must be reachable and bindable by one
  person with counsel, today — not contingent on a foundation accepting a project that has no adoption yet.

## A.1 Objective neutrality criteria (the admissibility contract for a holder)

These are the **necessary** conditions. A candidate holder is admissible only if it satisfies **all** of
N1–N7. They are written as testable predicates so the selection is re-derivable, not a matter of taste.

| # | Criterion | Test (pass/fail) | Why it is load-bearing (charter anchor) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **N1** | **Structural independence from the commercial entity** | Holder is a distinct legal person with no ownership, directorship, employment, or beneficial-interest link to the entity or its owner; not the owner, not the owner's wholly-controlled vehicle, not the owner's counsel-of-record acting as principal. | §2 ("neutral governance"); §7 (the failure mode is entity = mark-holder). A holder the owner controls is not a trustee — it is the owner wearing a hat. |
| **N2** | **No change-of-control conflict** | A future **acquisition / change of control of the entity (§4, item 1)** does not, by the holder's own constitution, change who controls the mark, the holder's incentives, or the holder's duty. The holder is not an asset that travels with the entity in a sale. | §4, item 1. If the holder is captured by the same event that triggers transfer, the trigger is hollow. |
| **N3** | **Capacity to transfer without entity consent** | The holder, under its constituting instrument, is **legally and operationally able** to execute the §4 transfer to the destination or a successor trustee on its own determination, **without** a signature, consent, or cooperation from the entity or its owner (including where the owner is unwilling, unreachable, deceased, or incapacitated). | §4 chapeau ("not subject to the commercial entity's consent"); §4 item-4 "reasonable determination". This is the single hardest criterion and the one that eliminates the most candidate forms. |
| **N4** | **Longevity beyond the entity (and beyond one person)** | The holder's existence and duty survive the death, dissolution, insolvency, or incapacity of the entity/owner; the holder has a defined succession path so the loss of any one principal does not orphan the mark. | §2 ("survive the commercial entity being acquired, pivoting, or dying"); §4 item 3 (abandonment). For a solo author, this is also key-person insurance for the standard. |
| **N5** | **Fiduciary / professional duty enforceable by an outsider** | The holder owes an enforceable duty (fiduciary, contractual, or professional-conduct) to administer the mark per the charter terms, breach of which a third party with standing (e.g., a trust beneficiary, a successor trustee, a regulator, or a bar authority) can act on — not merely the entity's goodwill. | §3 (irrevocability is worthless if no one can enforce it); §5/§6 (the public relies on the mark meaning what the charter says). |
| **N6** | **Determinacy of the transfer duty (a trigger machine the holder can run)** | The holder's obligations reduce to objective, checkable conditions (the §4 triggers) so the holder exercises defined judgment, not open-ended discretion, and is contractually equipped to receive and act on a third party's evidence that a trigger fired. | §4 (enumerated triggers); §5 (self-runnable, deterministic conformance is the cultural commitment — governance should match it). |
| **N7** | **Migration-compatibility with the §2 destination** | The holder can assign the mark onward to the independent-foundation destination **without re-litigating the terms** — i.e., it holds under terms a Linux-Foundation-style body can accept as-is, and assignment-onward is a permitted (indeed expected) act, not a breach of trust. | §2 ("assignment to an independent foundation is the migration target"); §3 (amendable only toward greater neutrality — onward assignment is the canonical permitted move). |

**Disqualifiers (any one is fatal):** the owner or owner's controlled vehicle as holder (fails N1/N3); a
holder whose authority lapses on the owner's death (fails N3/N4); a holder that needs the owner's
counter-signature to transfer (fails N3); a holder with discretion broad enough to *decline* to transfer
on a clear trigger to favor the entity (fails N5/N6).

## A.2 Admissible vehicle classes (the executable-today menu)

§2 fixes the *interim* vehicle as **trustee-escrow** and the *destination* as **foundation assignment**.
Between "do nothing" and "stand up a foundation," there are exactly four admissible classes executable by
one author with counsel today. Each is scored against N1–N7.

> All ratings below are **[ESTIMATE]** — a reasoned legal-structuring judgement, to be confirmed by the
> executing counsel in Part B. None is a measured value.

### Class A — Independent commercial escrow agent (software/IP escrow specialist)
A specialist escrow firm (the kind that holds source-code escrow for enterprise software deals) holds the
mark assignment + a transfer instrument in escrow, with release conditions = the §4 triggers.

- N1 ✅ structurally independent. N2 ✅ not captured by entity's change of control. N3 ⚠️ **conditional** —
  escrow agents are strong at *deterministic, evidenced* release conditions but classically weak at
  *discretionary* ones (the §4 item-4 "bad-faith capture… at the trustee's reasonable determination" is
  hard for a pure escrow agent to adjudicate). N4 ✅ firm longevity > one person. N5 ⚠️ duty is
  contractual, owed to the parties; outsider standing is thin. N6 ✅ for the objective triggers (§4 items
  1–3); ⚠️ for item 4. N7 ✅ assignment-onward is a clean release-to-named-beneficiary.
- **Verdict: admissible for the deterministic triggers; needs a determination agent for the item-4
  judgment.** Strong on "executable today," weak on judgment.

### Class B — Law-firm escrow / attorney-as-escrowee under written escrow agreement
A law firm holds the executed assignment in trust under an escrow agreement; release on §4 triggers. The
firm acts as a neutral escrowee bound by professional-conduct rules (client-trust / escrow-holding duties
enforceable by the bar).

- N1 ✅. N2 ✅. N3 ✅ — a firm can be empowered to execute a pre-signed assignment on a defined trigger
  without further owner signature, and to make the item-4 reasonable determination (lawyers routinely hold
  escrow-release discretion). N4 ⚠️ **partial** — a *firm* outlives a person, but a single named attorney
  does not; mitigated by naming the firm (not an individual) and a successor-escrowee clause. N5 ✅ —
  professional-conduct duty + escrow-agreement duty, bar-enforceable, outsider-visible. N6 ✅. N7 ✅.
- **Verdict: strongest "executable-today" all-rounder.** The only class that cleanly carries *both* the
  deterministic triggers *and* the item-4 discretionary one, while being bindable by one author + counsel
  in a single engagement.

### Class C — Interim trustee under a declaration/deed of trust (purpose/charitable-purpose trust)
The owner executes a deed of trust declaring the mark held on trust for the stated
charitable/standard-stewardship purpose, with a named trustee (individual or, better, a
corporate/professional trustee) and a successor-trustee mechanism. The §4 triggers are the trust's terms.

- N1 ✅. N2 ✅. N3 ✅ — a trustee's powers do **not** lapse on the settlor's death (the core advantage over
  any owner-dependent arrangement); the trustee transfers per the deed without the entity's consent.
  N4 ✅✅ — **best on longevity**: a corporate/professional trustee + successor mechanism is purpose-built
  to outlive any individual, including the settlor. N5 ✅✅ — **strongest enforceable duty**: trustees owe
  fiduciary duties; a purpose trust can name an enforcer with standing. N6 ✅. N7 ✅ — trustee assigns
  onward to the foundation as the trust's destination.
- **Verdict: strongest on the criteria that matter most for a solo author (N3/N4/N5), at the cost of more
  setup (deed drafting, possibly a corporate trustee engagement).** The natural one-step-from-foundation
  form.

### Class D — Existing neutral standards body / fiscal host takes the mark now
A neutral host (a software-freedom fiscal sponsor, or a foundation's incubation track) holds the mark
immediately — the §2 *destination* reached in one step, skipping the interim.

- All N-criteria ✅ in principle. **But:** §2 explicitly chose the interim vehicle *because* the
  destination is "wait on a foundation," and **[ASSUMPTION]** a foundation/host typically **will not accept
  a mark for a project with no adoption, no community, and a single contributor** — acceptance is gated on
  traction CAPAS does not yet have. *(This is an unsourced empirical generalization about
  LF/CNCF-style incubation gating — industry-standard but not a CAPAS-repo fact; counsel/the candidate host
  confirms.)* So this class **fails the "executable-today" posture in practice**, not on neutrality but on
  availability.
- **Verdict: the destination, not an interim option. Re-evaluate once adoption exists (see the
  migrate-to-foundation trigger, A.3).** Listing it keeps the path honest and prevents the interim vehicle
  from quietly becoming permanent.

**Class ranking on the executable-today axis:** B ≈ C (admissible and complete) > A (admissible but
incomplete on the item-4 judgment) > D (unavailable until adoption).

## A.3 The concrete designation (decision-ready)

Because the entity is a single natural person with no adoption yet, the designation must be **bindable
today, complete across all four §4 triggers, and survive the author's incapacity.** That eliminates A
(incomplete on item 4) and D (unavailable). The decision is between B and C, and the defensible answer is
to **combine them**: name the holder as a **professional escrowee under a deed-of-trust framing**, so the
holder has both an attorney-escrowee's executable-today readiness *and* a trustee's fiduciary durability.

### Named designation (primary)

> **§2 holder = a named independent law firm acting as escrow-trustee (Class B executed under a Class-C
> deed-of-trust framing), engaged specifically to hold the CAPAS certification mark in trust under the §4
> terms, with (i) a pre-signed onward-assignment instrument runnable on any §4 trigger without the owner's
> further consent, (ii) a named successor-escrowee, and (iii) a designated enforcer with standing to
> compel transfer.**

This is a **role-and-form designation**, deliberately not a brand name. **Reason it is correct *not* to
hard-name a specific firm here:** firm selection is the act of *engaging counsel*, which is Part B's
deliverable (drafting + executing the instrument). Picking the literal firm before counsel is engaged
would either (a) invent a name the charter cannot stand behind (no firm has been retained, so any named
firm would be an unsupported claim — regla cero), or (b) front-run the legal engagement. What this part
*can* and *does* fix decisively is the **form, the criteria the firm must meet, and the ranked basis for
selection** — exactly "WHO and WHY, decision-ready."

> **[ASSUMPTION] for counsel:** the B⊕C hybrid presumes an escrowee's release duties and a trustee's
> fiduciary duties are **mutually compatible in one engagement** in the governing jurisdiction (not merely
> that each is individually valid). If a jurisdiction treats the two roles as conflicting, fall back to a
> pure Class-C trust (rank 2) or Class-A-plus-adjunct (rank 3). The rank-1 pick is a defensible default,
> not a settled instrument.

### Selection rubric to instantiate the name (hand-off to the engagement)

The chosen firm/escrowee must satisfy, in priority order:

1. **N3 first** — demonstrable ability and willingness to hold a pre-signed assignment and execute it on a
   trigger *without* the settlor's further act (ask the candidate directly; if they cannot, they are
   disqualified regardless of reputation).
2. **N4 via the firm, not an individual** — engage the *firm* as escrowee with a successor clause; never a
   single named attorney as the sole holder.
3. **§4 item-4 competence** — willing to make the "reasonable determination" of bad-faith capture (this is
   what excludes a pure Class-A escrow agent).
4. **N7 onward-assignment** — comfortable that the trust's *purpose* is to assign onward to a foundation,
   i.e., the firm is a way-station, not the terminus.
5. **Jurisdictional fit** — domiciled where the mark is reserved/registered and where a
   purpose/charitable-purpose trust or equivalent escrow-with-fiduciary-duty is enforceable; mark-holding
   by a trustee must be valid there. *(Confirm in the engagement; flagged **[ASSUMPTION]** that the owner's
   home jurisdiction supports trustee-held marks — counsel verifies.)*

### Ranked shortlist of *vehicle instantiations* (defensible, in order)

| Rank | Instantiation | When to pick it | Criteria profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Independent law firm as escrow-trustee under a deed of trust** (B⊕C) | Default. One engagement gives executable-today readiness + fiduciary durability + item-4 judgment. | N1 ✅ N2 ✅ N3 ✅ N4 ✅(firm+successor) N5 ✅✅ N6 ✅ N7 ✅ |
| **2** | **Corporate/professional trustee under a stewardship/purpose trust** (C) | If counsel prefers a pure-trust form, or the home jurisdiction's purpose-trust law is strong, or B⊕C role-compatibility (above [ASSUMPTION]) does not hold. | Same profile; N4 strongest, slightly more setup. |
| **3** | **Specialist IP/software-escrow agent + a named determination agent for item-4** (A + adjunct) | If a trust is unavailable in jurisdiction and a firm-escrowee cannot be retained; the adjunct supplies the item-4 judgment escrow agents lack. | N1–N4, N6, N7 ✅; N5 ⚠️ thinner duty; needs the adjunct to close item 4. |
| — | **Neutral foundation / fiscal host (D)** | **Not an interim option — the destination.** Trigger to migrate below. | Admissible but unavailable pre-adoption. |

### The trigger that converts interim holder → §2 destination

To prevent the interim vehicle from silently becoming permanent (the failure that would let §2's
"destination" rot), bind the onward migration to an objective condition the holder already watches:

> **Migrate-to-foundation trigger:** the interim escrow-trustee initiates assignment to an independent
> foundation (§2 destination) upon the **first** of: (a) a qualifying neutral host's acceptance of the
> mark; or (b) CAPAS reaching a stated adoption threshold that makes a host willing to accept it. Until
> then the interim holder holds under the unchanged §4 terms. The specific adoption threshold and the
> host's acceptance criteria are **[DRAFT — to be set with counsel and the candidate host]**; this part
> fixes only that the migration is *triggered*, not discretionary, consistent with §3 (amendable only
> toward greater neutrality — onward assignment is the canonical permitted direction).

## B.2 Escrow and trust of the Mark *(operationalizes Charter §2)*

**B.2.1 Grant into escrow.** The Owner hereby assigns and delivers the Mark, together with all associated
goodwill (as required for a valid trademark assignment), **to the Trustee, to be held in trust** under this
Agreement. The Trustee holds legal title to the Mark for the limited purposes set out herein and for the

**B.2.2 What the Owner retains and what it does not.** The Owner retains: (a) copyright and the Apache-2.0

unaffected except as constrained by §B.3 and the T2 trigger); and (b) a non-exclusive, royalty-free,
non-revocable license to *use* the Mark in conformance with Charter §6 ("built with CAPAS" /

transfer, assignment, license, or sublicense of the Mark inconsistent with this Agreement; or (e) any right

Trustee.

**B.2.3 Trustee's standing duties.** The Trustee shall: (a) hold and not commercialize the Mark; (b) grant
and police the right-to-attest **only** through the Conformance Right (Charter §5/§6), neither widening nor
narrowing it except toward greater openness; (c) refuse any instruction that would constitute a Capture
Amendment (§B.3.2); and (d) upon a Trigger Event, execute the consent-free transfer of §B.4.

**B.2.4 Independence Criteria (for Trustee and any Successor Trustee).** The Trustee and any Successor
Trustee must, at the time of acting, satisfy criteria N1–N7 of Part A — in particular: (a) not be
controlled by, under common control with, or financially dependent on the Owner/Commercial Entity or any
acquirer; (b) have no commercial interest in capturing the standard; and (c) be willing to be bound by this
Agreement and the Charter §3 Ratchet.

**B.2.5 Destination precedence.** The migration target is assignment of the Mark to the Neutral Destination
(Charter §2). Until the Neutral Destination accepts assignment, the Trustee holds the Mark in trust.
Nothing here delays a §B.4 transfer pending Destination readiness: if no Neutral Destination is ready at a
Trigger Event, the Mark passes to a Successor Trustee (§B.4.2(b)) and onward to the Neutral Destination
when ready.

1. relicense the open core away from an OSI-approved license (Charter §1, §3);

5. introduce an Owner/Commercial-Entity consent requirement over a §B.4 transfer (Charter §4),

# Part C — §4 item-3 abandonment trigger: objective cure period

*Resolves charter Open item (3): the §4 item-3 "abandonment" trigger references "a stated cure period" but
states no value. This part makes the trigger objective and evidence-grounded — what counts as maintenance,
the measurement window, the cure period and its anchor, and who declares. It is subordinate to §3
(irrevocability) and to the Trustee's reasonable-determination standard (Charter §4, item 4), and is itself
a trigger/transfer term protected by §3 — amendable only toward greater neutrality, never to make
abandonment harder to declare.*

**C.0 Why an objective test.** A trigger that fires on the trustee's unaided impression is contestable and
slow; a trigger anchored to public, machine-checkable facts is the point of the instrument. Every signal
below is observable by a non-employee from the public repository, the published release feed, and the open
conformance suite — the same self-runnability the mark already rests on (§5). The trustee declares; the
trustee does not investigate from privileged access. Declaration is made under the trustee's
**reasonable-determination standard (Charter §4, trigger item 4)**, applied here by the same neutrality
logic.

**C.1 The three maintenance signals.** The engine/standard is **maintained** in a given window if **all
three** hold. Abandonment is the sustained failure of these signals, not the failure of any single one in
isolation.

| # | Signal | Objective, publicly observable test | Anchor / source |
|---|--------|--------------------------------------|-----------------|
| **M1** | **Conformance-suite green** | `python3 benchmarks/conformance.py` on the latest `main` exits success and reproduces the published verdict + `result_hash` (the same artifact a §5 self-runner gets). A suite that no longer passes on its own published release, or no longer runs, is the strongest abandonment signal: the standard can no longer attest itself. | §5 (self-run conformance, yearly re-cert); `benchmarks/conformance.py` |
| **M2** | **Security responsiveness** | A validly-submitted in-scope vulnerability (per `SECURITY.md` "What is in scope") receives the project's own committed **acknowledgment within 5 business days**. *[ASSUMPTION — disposition-within-cure-window]* A substantive disposition (fix, mitigation, or reasoned decline with a disclosure timeline) follows within the cure window (C.3). `SECURITY.md` commits to the 5-business-day acknowledgment and to agreeing a disclosure timeline; it does not itself commit to disposition within 90 days — the disposition-within-cure-window requirement is anchored to the **standard 90-day coordinated-disclosure norm** (C.3), not asserted from `SECURITY.md`. | `SECURITY.md` (5-business-day acknowledgment; fail-closed / determinism scope); 90-day coordinated-disclosure norm |
| **M3** | **Release / re-certification cadence** | At least one of: (a) a published release or a substantive merged maintenance commit to `main`, or (b) a completed yearly re-certification run, within the cadence window (C.2). | §5 and §8 (yearly re-certification; Sonobuoy/Certified-Kubernetes precedent) |

**C.2 Measurement window (cadence baseline): 12 months.** The cadence baseline for M3 is **twelve (12)
months of no qualifying activity**. This is derived, not invented: §8 adopts the **yearly re-certification**
norm of Certified Kubernetes / Sonobuoy as the precedent CAPAS follows, and §5 restates it ("yearly
re-certification to prevent stale forks"). The abandonment window therefore inherits **the annual
re-certification cadence the charter adopts as precedent (§5, §8)** — a project that has produced no
release, substantive maintenance commit, or re-certification in a full re-certification cycle has gone
stale against that cadence. M1 and M2 are evaluated continuously (a red suite or an ignored in-scope report
does not wait twelve months to become evidence); M3 supplies the 12-month baseline against which silence is
measured.

**C.3 Cure period: 90 days from notice. [ASSUMPTION — rationale below]** Once the trustee gives the
commercial entity **written notice of apparent abandonment** (citing which of M1/M2/M3 failed and the
supporting public evidence), the entity has a **ninety (90) day cure period** to restore the failed
signal(s) — e.g., publish a passing conformance run, ship a release or re-certification, or dispose of the
outstanding in-scope security report. Abandonment under §4 item 3 is declared only if the failure
**persists, uncured, past the close of this 90-day period**.

- **Rationale and anchor.** Ninety days is the convergent norm across the regimes this charter and
  `SECURITY.md` already operate in: (a) it matches the **standard 90-day coordinated-disclosure window**
  that the project's security posture lives inside, so the security signal (M2) and the cure clock share a
  calendar; (b) it sits within the cure-period band used by neutral-foundation governance for restoring a
  lapsed project before transfer, short enough to be meaningful against a 12-month cadence baseline yet
  long enough that a good-faith maintainer can demonstrably resume. It is tagged **[ASSUMPTION]** because
  no single cited artifact fixes "90 days" as a contractual cure period for CAPAS specifically; counsel
  executing the Part B instrument confirms or adjusts it against the chosen foundation's bylaws (Charter
  Open item (2)). The structure — notice → bounded cure → declaration — is the commitment; the exact number
  is the parameter counsel sets.
- **Stacking with the cadence baseline.** The 12-month cadence baseline (C.2) and the 90-day cure period
  (C.3) are sequential, not redundant: silence must first reach the 12-month baseline to make *apparent*
  abandonment, then survive a 90-day cured-or-not notice to become *declared* abandonment. Total elapsed
  minimum from last activity to a §4 item-3 transfer is therefore on the order of **fifteen (15) months**,
  deliberately erring toward the entity's benefit — abandonment is the one trigger where a false positive
  (declaring a merely-quiet-but-living project dead) is itself a capture risk, so the bar is high and the
  notice is mandatory.

**C.4 Who observes and declares.** Any party — not only the trustee — may surface evidence that M1/M2/M3
have failed, because every signal is publicly checkable. The **declaration**, however, is the trustee's
alone, exercised under the **trustee's reasonable-determination standard (Charter §4, trigger item 4),
applied here by the same neutrality logic**: the trustee confirms the failing signal against the public
evidence, issues the C.3 notice, observes the cure window, and — if the failure persists uncured — makes
the reasonable determination that abandonment has occurred, whereupon the §4 transfer to the neutral
destination proceeds **without the commercial entity's consent** (Charter §4 chapeau). The trustee's role
is confirm-notice-observe-declare against objective signals; it is not to second-guess the entity's
roadmap. As with the other triggers, the entity may have died quietly — §4 item 3 ensures the **standard
and its mark do not die with it.**
